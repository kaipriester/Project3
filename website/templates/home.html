Tropical Storm Detector

Analyzing NOAA Offshore Florida Buoy Data to Find Past Hurricanes

University of Florida COP 3530 Data Structures and Algorithms

 

Station 41009 (LLNR 840) - CANAVERAL 20 NM East of Cape Canaveral, FL Owned and maintained by National Data Buoy Center 3-meter foam buoy 28.508 N 80.185 W (28°30'27" N 80°11'6" W)

 

The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration’s (NOAA) moored buoys are essentially sea weather stations. These stations collect a massive amount of data on the hourly oceanographic environment. They transit data on the wave height and period, barometric pressure, temperatures, and wind. As you can imagine, this data is essential to meteorology, environmental studies, and climate sciences. One valuable data set is wind gust recordings. Wind gusts are great indicators of tropical storms. If there are recorded wind gusts greater than 75mph (65 knots) then the buoy is likely in a hurricane. The wind data from the buoy is recorded real time approximately every ten minutes. There is an archive of this data, and the goal is to capture the k greatest recorded wind gusts (maybe even ones greater than 75mph).
